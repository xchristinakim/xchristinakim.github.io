Vantive R&D

R&D internship at Vantive (formerly Baxter) working on next-generation renal care technologies. Details are under NDA.

Overview

As an R&D Exploratory & Innovation Engineering Intern, I worked on early-stage medical device innovation projects within a highly collaborative, multidisciplinary environment. My role involved investigating unmet user needs, translating insights into design opportunities, and rapidly developing and evaluating concepts through iterative prototyping and testing. Working closely with engineers, human factors specialists, designers, and commercial leads, I contributed to projects that balanced technical feasibility, clinical considerations, and user experience.

The Problem

Due to confidentiality agreements, specific project details cannot be disclosed. The challenge centered on addressing a complex healthcare need within a medical device context, where existing solutions presented opportunities for improvement in usability, workflow integration, user experience, and overall effectiveness. Like many exploratory R&D efforts, the problem space was initially ambiguous and required significant discovery work to better understand user needs, constraints, and opportunities before converging on potential solutions.

Design Process

Prototype Evaluation & User Testing

The project began with evaluating an existing prototype in an exploratory R&D environment where significant uncertainty remained around feasibility, usability, and market viability. To gather unbiased feedback, I designed and facilitated testing sessions with engineering interns who had no prior knowledge of the device, allowing us to identify intuitive interactions, points of confusion, and potential barriers to adoption. I also collaborated closely with Human Factors Engineers to assess usability considerations, user workflows, and opportunities for design improvement.


Requirements Definition & Risk Reduction

After synthesizing observations and feedback from testing, I developed a structured plan for design modifications and identified key project requirements necessary for success. Rather than focusing solely on improving the prototype itself, I helped define the criteria that would determine whether the project should continue moving forward. This included identifying and investigating critical "project killer" high-risk assumptions related to technical feasibility, user acceptance, implementation, and business viability that needed to be answered before additional resources were invested. This exploratory approach reduced uncertainty and ensured future development efforts were grounded in evidence rather than assumptions.


Systems Thinking

As the project progressed, I gained exposure to the complexity of designing medical devices for a global product ecosystem. While analyzing compatibility requirements, I discovered that the company manufactured numerous disposable product variants for different regions and markets around the world. These variations created challenges for developing a solution that could seamlessly integrate across all existing product lines and highlighted the importance of considering the broader system in addition to the device itself.


Business Opportunity Identification

The issue was not isolated to my project alone. Other engineering interns encountered similar difficulties when working on their own initiatives, and discussions with engineers revealed widespread uncertainty regarding which disposable sets existed, how they differed, and where they were used. Information was dispersed across multiple systems, making it time-consuming to identify, compare, and understand the full portfolio of disposables. The lack of visibility and standardization introduced unnecessary complexity into both product development and decision-making processes.

Recognizing that this challenge extended beyond the scope of a single project, I independently initiated a separate business case exploring the standardization of disposable product sets across the organization. The proposal was motivated by both the technical limitations encountered during development and the organizational inefficiencies observed across teams. By investigating opportunities to reduce product variation, improve compatibility, simplify development efforts, and lower manufacturing and operational costs, I highlighted how a more standardized approach could create value across engineering, manufacturing, and future innovation initiatives.

Learning Outcomes

Working in a medical device company gave me a much deeper understanding of the complexity behind developing healthcare technologies in a regulated, global environment. I learned that successful products are shaped by far more than technical performance alone; usability, clinical workflows, manufacturing constraints, regional product variations, regulatory considerations, business strategy, and patient safety all play critical roles in the development process. Even seemingly small design decisions can have downstream impacts across supply chains, training programs, product compatibility, and user adoption.

One of the most valuable aspects of the internship was experiencing how interdisciplinary medical device innovation truly is. Throughout the summer, I collaborated with engineers, human factors specialists, designers, project leaders, managers, and business stakeholders, each bringing a unique perspective to the decision-making process. I learned that solving complex healthcare challenges requires not only technical expertise, but also the ability to communicate effectively across disciplines, synthesize diverse viewpoints, and balance competing priorities.

The internship also reinforced the importance of exploratory engineering and innovation. I gained experience identifying assumptions, investigating critical uncertainties, defining project requirements, and using evidence-based decision-making to determine whether concepts were worth further investment. This experience strengthened my understanding that innovation is not simply about generating ideas—it is about systematically reducing uncertainty and creating confidence in future development decisions.

The experience culminated in presenting my project findings and recommendations to managers, team leaders, engineers, and senior leadership, including the Senior Director of Exploratory & Innovation and the Senior Vice President of Research & Development. These opportunities strengthened my ability to communicate technical insights clearly, tailor messages to different audiences, and advocate for strategic recommendations backed by data and research.

Overall, the internship allowed me to apply and expand many of the skills I developed at Northwestern, including human-centered design, research synthesis, systems thinking, prototyping, stakeholder engagement, and cross-functional collaboration. It provided invaluable exposure to real-world medical device development and confirmed my passion for working at the intersection of engineering, innovation, and healthcare. I am excited to return to Vantive in July 2026 as a full-time R&D Engineer on the Exploratory & Innovation team, where I will continue contributing to the development of future healthcare technologies.

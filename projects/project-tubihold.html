TubiHold

Non-invasive tube securement for pediatric cardiac patients — eliminating tape-based skin trauma through 7+ design iterations and clinical usability validation at Lurie Children's Hospital.

Overview

TubiHold is a non-invasive tube securement device designed to replace adhesive tape in pediatric cardiac intensive care settings. Developed in partnership with Lurie Children's Hospital, the device underwent 7+ design iterations grounded in clinical feedback, human factors research, and usability validation studies.

The Problem

Infants in the Cardiac Care Unit (CCU) at Ann & Robert H. Lurie Children's Hospital of Chicago often rely on nasogastric (NG) feeding tubes for nutritional support both during hospitalization and throughout long-term at-home care. Proper tube securement is critical — even slight tube migration can increase the risk of aspiration and airway obstruction.


However, current securement methods create significant challenges for both infants and caregivers:

  • Unintended tube removal caused by infants pulling at exposed tubing
  • Skin irritation and pressure injuries from prolonged adhesive use on sensitive infant skin
  • Moisture buildup from drool and vomit trapped beneath adhesives, worsening skin breakdown
  • Strangulation and safety risks from excess tubing left unsecured outside feeding times
  • Emotionally difficult tube changes for caregivers managing a moving, crying infant during replacement procedures

While NG tubes are essential for survival and recovery, the securement systems surrounding them often fail to account for infant anatomy, comfort, and the realities of long-term caregiving.

Design Process

Redefining the Problem Through Observation


The project originally began with a different focus. During our initial conversations with Ann & Robert H. Lurie Children's Hospital of Chicago, clinicians identified NG tube clogging as the primary issue. Formula and medication residue often became trapped within the grooves of the tubing, leading to discoloration and difficult flushing procedures. However, after conducting hospital walkthroughs, observing patient care environments, and speaking directly with clinicians and parents, our team uncovered a more urgent and human-centered problem. We repeatedly noticed infants with visibly irritated, red, and scarred cheeks caused by adhesive securement methods. Moisture from drool and vomit became trapped beneath medical tape, accelerating skin breakdown and discomfort. At the same time, parents described the constant challenge of keeping tubes secure while preventing their children from pulling them out. This shift in perspective fundamentally changed the direction of the project. Rather than focusing on the tube itself, we focused on the experience of living with the tube.



Human Factors & Infant Anatomy


In early ideation, our team explored multiple securement concepts inspired by:

  • Oxygen masks
  • Head wraps
  • Wearable soft-good systems

Through usability studies and clinician feedback, we quickly learned that designing for infants required a completely different approach than designing for adults. Infant skulls are highly malleable, and concentrated pressure around the ears, nose, or head can lead to deformation and pressure injuries. This insight became a major turning point in the project. Our team shifted toward creating a system that distributed force across multiple contact points rather than relying on a single high-pressure anchor area. Multiple iterations were developed to improve comfort, adjustability, and long-term wearability.



Caregiver-Centered Design


One of the most impactful parts of the project came from working directly with caregivers.


Although our team had already received NG tube insertion training using simulation dolls, observing real caregivers revealed the emotional and physical complexity of the process. Parents were not simply replacing a tube — they were caring for a medically vulnerable child who was often crying, moving, and resisting the procedure.


These interactions pushed us to rethink usability entirely:

  • How could the system remain secure while simplifying tube changes?
  • How could it work across different feeding and sleeping positions?
  • How could caregivers operate the device quickly under stress?

These insights led us to prioritize intuitive adjustment mechanisms, simplified handling, and flexibility during both feeding and replacement procedures.



Iteration, Testing & Engineering Constraints


As the project progressed, our team collaborated with professionals across the medical device industry, including: Human factors engineers, Medical device product engineers, and Manufacturing engineers.


These discussions helped shape our approach to:

  • Safety testing
  • Usability Validation
  • Performance Testing
  • Manufacturability Considerations

A major challenge was validating the concept without the ability to test directly on infants. To overcome this limitation, our team developed scaled prototypes for self-testing and conducted extensive literature reviews on pediatric and infant medical device standards. This allowed us to collect preliminary performance and safety data while refining the design.

Final Design Features

Adjustable Dual-Point Securement


The design incorporates two independent adjustability points:

  1. Tube Holder Adjustment — allows clinicians and caregivers to customize how the tube holder interfaces with the infant’s face
  2. Headband Adjustment — provides adaptable fit across different infant head sizes while reducing concentrated pressure points

Reduced Adhesive Dependency


The system minimizes direct adhesive contact with the infant’s skin, helping reduce irritation, redness, and pressure injuries.


Tubing Management


Excess tubing can be secured and contained during non-feeding periods, reducing entanglement and strangulation risks.



Soft-Goods Wearable Design


The device functions as a wearable support system designed specifically around infant anatomy and comfort.



Outcomes

Our team continued developing the project beyond the academic design cycle. As of May 2026:

  • A provisional patent has been filed
  • The team has begun drafting the full patent application with legal counsel
  • Advanced the concept beyond an academic prototype toward potential commercialization
  • Began pitching the technology for licensing opportunities with major infant medical device manufacturers such as Neotech Products LLC
